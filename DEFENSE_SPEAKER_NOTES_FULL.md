### Slide 03 · Manual reality today

Let me show you what orchestration actually looks like today. On the left you see the operator's stack: four layers running across the far edge, regional clusters, and the cloud. At the top, in layer one, the operator sets intent — for example, *"reduce energy while keeping SLA"* — and then spends hours in dashboards and tribal knowledge trying to translate that sentence into concrete commands. Below, in layer two, there is no intelligent component that bridges natural-language intent and the control plane, so the operator must do that translation by hand. In layer three, where prediction should close the loop, no accurate per-node forecast exists at the far edge, and in the regional cluster a new model has to be refit for every service. Layer four, the infrastructure itself, is simply the given reality — heterogeneous devices, distributed across providers, running whatever services the business demands. Four layers, four open problems, and one operator holding it all together by hand.

### Slide 04 · Zero-touch: operator sets intent, not actions

The goal of this thesis is to replace that manual reality with a zero-touch closed loop. The operator sets intent — goals, SLAs, constraints — and the loop does the rest. You see the four stages on the left: predict, decide, act, observe. A forecast feeds a decision, the decision is executed, the new state is observed, and fresh traces flow back into the next prediction. Operator review happens only on exceptions, not on every action. On the right I want to convince you *why* the predict stage is load-bearing, because if you remove prediction, the whole loop collapses into reactive scaling, and reactive scaling pays the price twice. Picture the black line as demand: baseline at idle, a spike comes in, plateau at peak, and then it releases back to idle. The red staircase is what a reactive controller actually does — it only reacts *after* demand has already climbed, so during the ramp-up the red curve is below the black curve; that red-shaded gap is SLA violations, users queuing, requests timing out, because we are late to scale up. Then on the other side, when the spike has already released, the reactive controller is still sitting at peak capacity — it has no reason yet to scale down — so the amber-shaded gap is wasted energy, nodes burning watts to serve traffic that is not there anymore; late to scale down. The dashed cyan line is the predictive alternative: capacity is already in place when the demand arrives, and released as soon as it leaves. Prediction closes *both* gaps at once, which is why it sits at the top of the loop — it is what lets decide, act and observe do their jobs on time. Each of the three contributions I defend today closes one of the openings in this loop; prediction is where two of them land.

### Slide 05 · Forecasting must run where the service runs

The first open problem is prediction at the edge, and the tension is easy to state in numbers. On one side you have today's SOTA in workload forecasting — a transformer like Pathformer at 2.4 million parameters. It is accurate, but it is simply too big and too slow to run where the service runs. On the other side you have lightweight time-series models at around fifty thousand parameters. They are deployable, but their accuracy collapses the moment the workload shifts. That is the deployability gap: the quadrant that is both small and adaptive is empty. This leads directly to the first research question: *can workload prediction remain practical and accurate at edge scale?* I put the target on the slide up front: fewer than one thousand parameters, with competitive accuracy.

### Slide 06 · One model for many services

The second open problem is generalisation. Even if the first problem were solved, today's forecasters are trained per service, and the cost of that scales as *O(N)*. Every new service, and every drift event, requires a new retraining, a new monitoring pipeline, a new tuning cycle. At 6G scale — a typical telco operator running three hundred or more microservices — that is not a maintenance strategy, it is an impossible operator workload. So the second research question is *can one forecasting framework cover heterogeneous service workloads without per-service retraining?* The critical observation on this slide is that the scalability barrier is retraining burden, not inference cost, and that changes what the solution has to look like.

### Slide 07 · From natural-language intent to intelligent orchestration action

The third and fourth problems live at the decision layer, and they are best illustrated by tracing a single sentence. The operator types *"reduce energy while keeping SLA."* Today, the bridge between that sentence and the concrete cluster actions — `kubectl drain`, scale, migrate, power-off — is a human being, reading dashboards, picking services, picking targets, picking commands. There is simply no component that reads natural-language intent and turns it into an executable plan, so the operator fills the gap manually, and the system scales with human attention rather than with the workload. Natural-language intent is not a stylistic preference; it is a formal input requirement, and it rules out heuristics, reinforcement learning, and mixed-integer linear programming, which cannot consume a sentence. That leads to the third and fourth research questions, which I treat together: *can natural-language intent be turned directly into validated orchestration actions, without a human in the loop?* The decision layer must both understand intent and act on it safely. That is what zero-touch actually means.

### Slide 12 · Adaptive periodicity in three steps

AERO's mechanism is three stages, in order. First, period detection: an online autocorrelation estimator finds the dominant repeat cycle τ in the trace, and re-estimates it every window. Second, adaptive forecasting: the trace is reshaped around the detected τ and handed to a lightweight SparseTSF-style head, fewer than six hundred parameters, less than half a millisecond of inference. Third, layer caching: the head's layers are cached per period — the examples on the slide show τ equal to twenty-four hours, twelve hours and six hours — and the cache reuses layers on a hit, which is what keeps inference inside the budget even when the signal shifts. Three stages, no attention, no KV cache, no per-service fine-tuning. From here we have two questions to answer in the experiments: is AERO actually efficient on standard benchmarks, and does a better forecast change the controller's behaviour?

### Slide 23 · How OmniFORE generalizes in three phases

This slide now reads left to right. First, `Designing the training set`, stages S₁ through S₄: encode traces into a latent space, cluster similar shapes, then sample a representative catalogue instead of picking traces at random. Second, `Training the model`, stages S₅ and S₆: rescale each service so no workload dominates, then use attention to learn what matters across the shared window. That is how one forecaster can serve many heterogeneous services. Third, `Tuning so it transfers`, stage S₇: choose hyperparameters on held-out services, so the winner is the setting that transfers, not the setting that only fits what it has already seen. The next three slides zoom into those three cards.

### Slide 24 · Designing the training set

Phase one is where representativeness is engineered rather than wished for. The revised slide makes the hand-off explicit visually: raw traces become fingerprints, fingerprints become families, families become labels, and labels become a balanced mix. S₁ finds a fingerprint for each trace, a compact set of shape features that captures how the workload actually moves. S₂ groups traces by shape into three operational families — bursty, steady, and periodic. S₃ tags every trace with its family, so the dataset is labelled. S₄ builds a balanced mix, proportional in the right way to the catalogue, so that no single family dominates the training signal. The point of this slide is the curation pipeline itself: by the end of phase one, the model will see a balanced catalogue instead of an arbitrary sample. The next slide picks up from there and explains how attention uses that curated set.

### Slide 25 · Same scale, then focus

Phase two still does two jobs: S₅ is equal scale, and S₆ is efficient attention. S₅ rescales every trace individually so that every service lands on equal footing, which means the optimiser weighs a quiet batch service just as seriously as a noisy web front-end. S₆ scans the full recent window, keeps the strongest matches, and forecasts from those links. That transfers well because the model matches patterns rather than service identity, so after rescaling it can carry what it learned into unseen workloads.

### Slide 26 · Tuning so it transfers

Phase three is where we decide what "good" actually means. Bayesian optimisation scans the hyperparameter space with a Gaussian-process surrogate and an acquisition function that picks the next configuration to try. The critical design decision is what we *score* each configuration against. The usual practice is to score against the training distribution, which rewards memorising the training set. OmniFORE scores against a held-out batch of services that was kept out of training entirely, so the optimiser is rewarded for choosing hyperparameters that work on services the model has never seen. Generalisation is enforced at the optimiser level, not only at the dataset level.

### Slide 27 · Does picking smart training data really help?

The first experiment is an ablation on the pipeline itself. Version A is the OmniFORE way — one representative trace per cluster, selected from bursty, steady, and periodic families. The reason this experiment is worth running is that it isolates the training-set design from the model, so any difference in the results is attributable to the pipeline, not to the head.

### Slide 36 · Each pain-point forces exactly one design decision

The rationale table has four rows, and each row is the minimal response to one specific pain-point. ### Slide 39 · How the agent plans, simulates, and executes safely

This is the core of AgentEdge. The Planning agent runs a five-phase loop: strategic planning, state analysis, action selection, digital-twin simulation with a critic, and a check on whether the intent is satisfied. If the critic rejects the simulated outcome, control returns to action selection rather than being committed to the cluster. Four technical mechanisms sit underneath that loop: tool-call batching, a three-tier state representation, rejection-feedback on failed plans, and deadlock detection. The example JSON at the bottom of the slide shows the output of a successful cycle — a plan object with `validated: true`, an energy delta of minus eighteen percent, and SLA preserved. Nothing reaches the cluster until that object exists. Validation before execution is structural, not a post-hoc check.

### Slide 43 · Is the twin structural or decorative?

The ablation turns the digital twin on and off and holds everything else constant. The *WITH* arm is the full AgentEdge — Plan, Simulate, Critique, Execute. The *WITHOUT* arm runs the same four agents, with planning and critique, but strips out the simulation step so the critic has no simulated trajectory to evaluate. Let me be precise about what "WITHOUT" means here, because this is a question that often comes up: on a Crit failure the plan is refined *in place* by the Exec agent; no round-trip back to Planning. In other words, the twin's contribution is isolated as *pre-execution validation*, not as the existence of a retry mechanism at all. The Q&A anticipation on-slide is the related point: the twin does not change *whether* a plan can succeed, it changes *how many physical retries* it takes. We report both success rate and API-call count, so failure-shifting cannot hide behind either metric alone.

### Slide 44 · Sandbox validation reduces costly trial-and-error

The ablation tells a very direct story. With the twin, success is 78.3 percent; without it, success drops to 53.3 percent — a 1.47 times improvement. The more operational number is API behaviour. With the twin, API-call counts stay tight at 10.9 with a standard deviation of 1.1. Without the twin, they range from eight to five-hundred-and-seventeen with a standard deviation of 109.4 — about ten times higher variability. Without simulation, the agent enters unproductive retry loops that are not just slower; they are operationally unsafe, because every retry touches production. The twin is therefore structural: it lifts success *and* collapses variance. The last experiment asks how this behaviour changes as the infrastructure grows.

### Slide 48 · Future work · agentic era

The same stack appears one more time, but now layers three and four are dimmed and explicitly marked *solved*, while layer one and layer two stay lit. ### Slide 49 · Benchmarks do not exist yet

Let me start with the first trust problem: benchmarking. At the moment, we simply do not have benchmark suites for agentic orchestration, and without them developers cannot know whether a change helps or hurts. The reason this is hard is that these tasks rarely have one ground-truth label; several plans can be valid at once. On top of that, LLM non-determinism means a fix that improves one scenario can break another. And if we had good benchmark data, it would not only improve evaluation, it would also enable fine-tuning smaller, orchestration-specific models. So trust begins with evaluation engineering. Before these systems can be deployed confidently, the field needs shared test suites, and research should characterise *equivalence classes* of correct orchestration plans; expand LLM evaluation theory into orchestration; and build orchestration-trace datasets — plan, state, outcome — that enable small LoRA/SFT models that are cheaper and easier to audit.

### Slide 50 · Latencies accumulate to tens of seconds

The second barrier is real-time performance. Even simple orchestration tasks can take tens of seconds because the planner iterates, calls tools, revises, and reasons again, and token costs compound at scale. This is ultimately a context-engineering problem, not a hardware one. The three barriers on this slide: first, the state the agent sees when reasoning starts may already differ by the time it executes. Second, accuracy drops once the tool surface gets too large — in our experiments, beyond roughly thirty tools, and in some cases removing tools actually improved results. Third, latency points toward the same lesson AERO taught us at the prediction layer: smaller, fine-tuned models may be the only practical way to break the latency barrier. State summarisation, tool sharding, retrieval-over-state; specialised models per decision — scale, migrate, placement; and, further out, agent loops fused with the RAN and core on sub-millisecond radio loops, unifying signal-theory timing budgets with LLM inference budgets.

### Slide 51 · Parallel instances conflict on shared infra

The third direction is system architecture at scale. Once orchestration is deployed across regions, providers, or administrative domains, the problem is no longer one agent in one cluster; it is many agent instances acting on shared infrastructure. The slide gives three open questions. First, the same prompt routed through a different model or provider can break because the output format shifts; we need an OpenAPI-equivalent for agent tool-calls, so that swapping provider X for provider Y preserves agent behaviour. Second, consensus or lock-lease schemes for *intended* actions, so conflicts are detected *before* actuation, not after. And third, scaling will come from parallelising across regions, not from adding more agents in sequence. Without these protocols, one agent may consolidate onto a node while another powers it down.

### Slide 52 · Three layers · one systems thesis

Three claims, each defended with three anchor numbers, nine anchors in total. Claim one, AERO — 599 parameters, eight-times-lower error under drift, and fifty-percent fewer migrations than SOTA: edge-real-time prediction is feasible with a sub-one-thousand-parameter model, with accuracy preserved. Claim two, OmniFORE — 30.41 percent lower MAE versus ModernTCN, a 20.66 percent MAE gain from clustering, and zero-shot deployment on an unseen cloud: a single attention-based model generalises across services, no per-service retraining. Claim three, AgentEdge — 1.47 times twin-on versus twin-off, ten-times-lower API-call variance, and 300.8 watts peak savings at eighteen nodes: validated autonomous orchestration — planning plus twin plus critic — is reliable and saves energy. The banner quote captures the thesis in one line: *orchestration turned from reactive supervision into a proactive, validated control stack; across three layers, with nine anchor numbers to defend it.* That is what I defend.
